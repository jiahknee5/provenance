Ordered by fastest, safest, lowest-effort first. Each is framed as a business change; open "how it's wired" for the engineering.

For clinical-ops buyers, test a headline that opens with the proof — "Reduce average length of stay by 1.2 days" — instead of a soft promise. It uses a claim we've already verified, so it's safe by construction.

Quick win

3

Switch on self-optimization for the education funnel

Step-change

The education landing buttons are fixed text today. Make them something the engine can test and improve. This is the highest-leverage move in the plan — the funnel starts getting better on its own, and it's the audience where the evidence transfers best.

Liftslanding → signupRealistic+5–7%EffortMRiskLow
How it's wired
Convert the per-archetype CTA from a fixed string to a small set of options the engine selects from, rewarded on the signup click that's already logged. This stands up the education bandit for the first time.
4

Start measuring the entry gate at all

Fix the blind spot

The email gate isn't even tracked today, so we can't tell how many people get through it. Add the tracking first, then test a clearer button ("Build my page" instead of "Go"). The real win is finally being able to see — and improve — the very top of the funnel.

Liftsemail-captureRealistic+3–12%EffortMRiskLow
How it's wired
Add an enter-rate event to the gate, then a small button-text test on top. The new copy makes no factual claim, so it clears the Gate trivially.
5

Put your trust badges where the decision happens

Quick win

Show SOC 2 / HIPAA / "deployed across 12 systems" right next to the lead form — these are real, verified claims, and SOC 2 is a genuine procurement gate for health systems. Strongest for security and quality buyers.

Liftsform completionRealistic+5–8%EffortMRiskLow
How it's wired
A proof strip sourced from the same verified claims the product already uses; the engine learns which proof to lead with per buyer. Nothing un-provable can appear.
6

Reassure at the submit button — but test the wording carefully

Guardrail test

Add a short reassurance line above submit. Test positive framing ("2 min · no sales call") against a "we never sell your data" line — because negative wording like that has been measured cutting conversion ~19% elsewhere. The point of this test is to catch the version that backfires before it ships.

Liftsform completionRealistic−5 to +10%EffortSRiskMedium
How it's wired
Three variants {none, positive, "never sell"} as a context feature, each routed through the Gate so the wording must stay operationally true of the flow.

One rule across all six: reward the engine on the deepest signal you trust (a booked assessment or enrollment over a raw click), so it never learns to win a vanity metric at the funnel's expense.

3 · Why you can trust these numbers
We deflate the hype before you spend a sprint on it

This is the same discipline that keeps the product honest, pointed at our own growth roadmap: nothing goes on the list that we can't stand behind.

The honesty checkWe didn't just brainstorm 68 ideas — we adversarially fact-checked every high-stakes one. The famous "+90% from one word" CRO numbers are business-to-consumer outliers or mis-attributed; the chart below shows what was claimed versus what's realistic for hospital buyers and career-switchers. The gap is the hype we removed so you don't pay for it in a wasted test.

Fact-checking cut 6 of 68 ideas outright and pulled nearly every remaining lift down to low single digits. Most-deflated: the classic first-person-button and field-cut claims — real in retail checkout, largely absent on a captive sign-up gate or a multi-stakeholder hospital form.

The most over-claimed idea promised +8–25% from a "free · no-obligation · 15-minute" assessment button — cut to zero, because none of that is true (the product is priced per record and the "15 minutes" was invented). We won't ship a claim we can't back, even to win a click.

The 6 ideas we cut — and why. Each one would have put a claim in front of a customer that we can't prove. That's the same line the product itself never crosses.

The ideaWhy we cut it
"Free, 15-min, no-obligation" buttonThe product is priced per record and the 15-minute figure was invented — none of it is provable.
"The info you already shared"Implies the visitor gave us consent, but some of that data was purchased / enriched — a claim we don't have.
"Shorten your patients' stays by 1.2 days"Turns an average result into a personal promise to one reader — over-claiming.
"N independent sources on file"The count was inflated (claims share sources), and self-rendered receipts aren't truly "independent."
"92% finish — across 600+ enrollments"Mixes two different denominators (graduates hired vs. students enrolled) — not a clean stat.
"We'll be in touch within one business day"An SLA the operations team may not actually hit; softened to "shortly."
4 · The one big idea
Transparency is a dial, not a free win

Showing your proof builds trust — up to a point. Past that point it backfires. The engine should find the right setting per audience, and the creepy setting should be locked out of reach.

Turning the dial up helps with cautious buyers. Showing the verified sources behind each claim reassures risk-averse hospital buyers — it's third-party-style proof a plain marketing sentence isn't. Worth roughly +3–12% for those segments.
Turning it too far backfires. Telling a visitor "we noticed you're a [title] at [company] — even though you never filled out a form" reads as surveillance and depresses conversion. It's the one setting that crosses from "helpful" to "creepy."

The control: let the engine optimize the safe range of personalization, and lock the "creepy" setting out of reach entirely — the same way the product makes an unprovable claim unreachable, not merely discouraged. Reward on the booking or enrollment, with a penalty for unsubscribes, so a creepy-but-clicky variant gets punished, not rewarded.

▸ Appendix — how this list was produced

Fan-out: a research swarm of 10 parallel lanes proposed 68 ship-ready tests — 5 covering specific pages (entry gate, education landing, Helix form, Helix page, thank-you/booked) and 5 covering cross-cutting levers (headlines, buttons, form design, social proof, and the transparency dial).

Fact-check: every high-impact idea was handed to a skeptic whose job was to disprove it — checking the cited evidence is real (1,057 source look-ups), that it transfers to our buyers rather than retail shoppers, and that the wording stays inside the product's truth boundary. Result: 6 cut, nearly all lifts corrected down.

Why the deflation: public conversion-rate evidence is overwhelmingly retail/e-commerce; the headline numbers are single-test outliers or mis-attributed. Our buyers are multi-stakeholder, risk-averse health systems and self-selected career-switchers — so the honest expectation is smaller, compounding wins.
