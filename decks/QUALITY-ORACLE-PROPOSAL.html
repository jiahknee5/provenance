The Quality Oracle

A small model reads a big model's mind — and tells you, before you pay, whether to trust the answer.

Thesis. LLMs often encode that they're about to be wrong in their intermediate activations — then discard that signal at the output layer. We train a small probe to read those hidden states and predict correctness during generation: a calibrated quality oracle that routes cheap-vs-expensive, gates hallucinations, and cascades models. Neither the LLM (it can't reliably self-judge) nor classical ML (it can't generate) does this alone.

The reframe (the spiky POV)

The model already knows it's wrong. We just stopped listening.

Everyone asks the LLM "are you sure?" — and trusts the words it generates back. But self-verbalized confidence is poorly calibrated and falls apart under distribution shift. Meanwhile the uncertainty signal lives in the intermediate layers and gets thrown away by the final token projection. Production routers ignore all of it and fall back to length/keyword heuristics. We stop asking the model to grade itself and instead read the activations directly.

The unit of progress: accuracy at a fixed dollar cost. A calibrated oracle lets you spend the expensive model only on the queries that need it — and abstain on the ones nobody should trust.
1

The problem — and why it's technically hard

"Predict whether an answer is correct before you commit to it" sounds simple and is genuinely unsolved. The hard parts:

  • Calibration under distribution shift — the named open problem. The 2026 routing survey flags that quality estimators "struggle with distribution shift" and that self-reported uncertainty is poorly aligned with actual correctness. A probe that's calibrated on one task can decalibrate badly on another; closing that is the research edge.
  • The signal is in the middle, not the output. Final-layer logits collapse the model's uncertainty into a token distribution. Evidence of "about to hallucinate" sits in intermediate hidden states — so you must find which layers and token positions carry it (a feature-selection problem over a high-dimensional activation tensor).
  • You need correctness labels to train. A probe is supervised — it needs a labeled "this generation was right/wrong" set, which only exists cleanly in checkable domains. Bootstrapping there and generalizing out is the whole game.
  • It has to be cheaper than the alternatives it beats. Self-consistency (sample N, vote) and judge ensembles already estimate confidence — but cost N× forward passes. A probe is one forward pass on activations you already computed. The bet only matters if the probe matches or beats those at a fraction of the cost.
  • Cascades compound the difficulty. One-stage routing is studied; multi-stage cascades with a learned accept/reject head per stage (small → mid → big), jointly optimized for cost-at-fixed-quality, are explicitly under-explored.

Ambition lives in turning model introspection into a calibrated, transferable, cheaper-than-sampling controller — and proving it on the cost-accuracy frontier.

2

Project direction

Primary fit · Direction A

Combine ML with LLM applications. A trained probe/regressor (classical ML) does the one thing the LLM cannot — reliably know when it is wrong — and the LLM does the generation. The probe controls the LLM at runtime (accept / escalate / abstain). This is the textbook "each does what it's best at," with the coupling being load-bearing: remove the probe and you're back to crude heuristics or paying the big model every time.

Why not the other framings

  • One problem, many ways (C) — we will benchmark the probe against self-consistency, semantic entropy, and LLM-judge confidence, but the project's spine is building the trained internals-probe + cascade, not a method survey. Lead with A.
  • Agent design (B) — the oracle is a component an agent could use (when to think / escalate), but our deliverable is the predictor and its frontier, not an agent architecture.
3

AI is the primitive, not a bolt-on

The tell: the product is a trained model that reads another model's internals. There is no version of this that's "an LLM with a prompt" — the core artifact is a probe fit to hidden-state features and a calibrated routing/cascade policy on top of it.

query ─► OPEN MODEL (generate draft + expose hidden states) │ activations hₗ at selected layers / token positions ▼ QUALITY ORACLE (trained probe) ─► p(correct), calibrated → low ECE │ ▼ ROUTER / CASCADE (risk-coverage policy) ├─ predicted-correct ──► accept the cheap answer (most queries, ~$) ├─ uncertain ──────► escalate to BIG / next stage ($$) └─ predicted-wrong ───► regenerate / abstain / flag │ labeled correctness set ────────┘ (ground-truth domain → train + calibrate + measure)
  • Activation extraction. Run an open-weights model; capture intermediate hidden states. Sweep layers/token positions to find where the correctness signal concentrates (probing literature: middle layers retain uncertainty the output discards).
  • The probe. A lightweight head (logistic → small MLP) maps selected activations to p(correct), then temperature/isotonic calibration. We report AUC, ECE, and reliability diagrams — calibration is a first-class metric, not an afterthought.
  • Router / cascade. Walk the risk-coverage curve: accept the confident tail, escalate the uncertain middle to a bigger model (or next cascade stage with its own learned accept head), abstain on predicted-wrong. Optimize accuracy at fixed cost.
  • Honest baselines. Every claim is measured against self-verbalized confidence, a length/perplexity heuristic, self-consistency (N-sample voting), and semantic entropy — with cost accounted for, because the probe's edge is being a single forward pass.
4

Scope — what "working" means in two weeks

Anchor in a domain with automatic correctness (math with checkable answers, code with hidden tests, or QA with a gold set) so the probe has clean labels and the frontier is honestly measurable.

Minimum shippable — must hit
  • One open-weights model + one ground-truth dataset; activation extraction pipeline working.
  • A trained probe on a single good layer predicting correctness, with calibration (ECE + reliability diagram).
  • A router that escalates predicted-failures to a big model.
  • The headline chart — cost-accuracy frontier: Oracle-routed vs. self-confidence vs. length heuristic vs. always-small / always-big. "Big-model accuracy at a fraction of the cost."
Ambitious — scored on ambition
  • Layer/token sweep + multi-layer fusion — locate and combine the signal across the network.
  • Distribution-shift test — train on domain A, test calibration on B. This is the open problem, stated and measured.
  • Inline hallucination gate — abstain/flag mid-generation, not just post-hoc routing.
  • Multi-stage cascade with learned per-stage accept heads (small → mid → big), beating single-stage routing on cost-at-quality.
  • Probe vs. sampling, head-to-head — show internals match/beat self-consistency & semantic entropy at a fraction of the compute.

Why it's compelling — the recruit hook

  • It's an x-ray of the model. "We can look inside an LLM and predict its mistakes before it finishes the sentence" is a magnet for the ML-leaning, internals-curious teammates you want.
  • The win is crisp and measurable: a single cost-accuracy curve where your line dominates — "same accuracy, ~40% of the cost" or "catch 90% of hallucinations for one extra forward pass."
  • Densest AI-engineering credential on the menu. Probing internals, calibration/ECE, selective prediction, routing & cascades — exactly the skills that read as authority to any serious AI team.

Hard parts / risks — the bet and where it breaks

The bet: a probe on hidden states predicts correctness better-calibrated and far cheaper than asking the model or sampling it — and that calibration transfers across tasks.

RiskWhere it breaks · mitigation
Transfer / decalibration
(top risk)		The probe may decalibrate badly off its training distribution — the named open problem. Mitigation: make the shift experiment a headline deliverable; multi-layer fusion + recalibration on a small target sample; report honestly where it degrades rather than hide it.
Must beat sampling at lower costIf a probe only ties self-consistency, the cost argument is the whole point — so it must be argued with real compute accounting. Mitigation: always plot accuracy per dollar/FLOP, not just accuracy; the probe's one-forward-pass edge is the thesis.
Signal may be absent on hard tasks"Jagged intelligence" — the model may be confidently wrong with no internal tell. Mitigation: choose tasks with headroom; characterize where the probe works vs. fails as a finding, not a failure.
Label scarcityProbe is supervised; needs correctness labels. Mitigation: auto-checkable domains (math/code/QA-with-gold) give cheap labels; semi-supervised extension if time allows.
Open-weights dependencyReading activations needs open models. Mitigation: that's the project's home turf; a closed-model fallback (logits + entropy only) is weaker and noted as such, not relied on.
Overfitting the probeTiny head can memorize a dataset's quirks. Mitigation: held-out + cross-domain eval, reliability diagrams, and a length/perplexity baseline to prove the probe adds real signal.
