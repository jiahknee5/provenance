Agency deck · technical appendix

Technical depth per phase — and the research that makes it frontier

The companion to the agency deck for a technical or research-savvy reader. For each phase: what's genuinely hard, the current research that shows it's unsolved, and an honest maturity rating — applied · emerging · frontier.

Where the defensible AI ambition sits

Not every phase is bleeding-edge, and pretending otherwise is what a sharp reviewer punishes. The frontier weight is concentrated in Phase 3 (the verifier), Phase 5 (truth-bounded optimization), and Phase 6 (reliability assurance) — the hottest current research cluster, trust & verification. Phases 1–2 have a real but more mature edge; Phase 4 is deliberately systems engineering, not a research claim. The single most original idea is Phase 5's constrained optimization as a structural fix for reward-hacking.

Phase by phase

PHASE 1Plan & Target emerging
Technical depth

Turn messy company docs into a structured, queryable claim/fact store + source map (claim extraction + normalization into an ontology), and cluster prospects into hundreds of micro-segments via embeddings.

Why it's at the edge

"Turn ugly documents into reliable knowledge" is genuinely unsettled — retrieval and generation interact in ways no one has nailed.

Anthropic — Contextual Retrieval: large reductions in failed retrieval by embedding chunk-level context; still a vendor technique, not a solved science.
DeepRead: argues agentic search still treats docs as flat chunks; structure-aware "locate-then-read" yields ~10% gains — i.e., the default is leaving accuracy on the table.
GraphRAG analyses + RAG-eval survey: GraphRAG often underperforms vanilla RAG; evaluation is hard because retrieval and generation are coupled.
PHASE 2Create — the ultra-personalization engine emerging
Technical depth

Per-prospect, retrieval-grounded generation constrained to approved claims — controllable, faithful generation conditioned on a prospect feature vector, not free-form copywriting.

Why it's at the edge

Faithfulness and context engineering are open: getting a model to use only the provided evidence, reliably, is harder than it looks.

EMNLP 2025 — "Context Length Alone Hurts LLM Performance Despite Perfect Retrieval": models degrade as context grows even when retrieval is perfect — so "just put the claims in the prompt" doesn't guarantee faithful use.
Anthropic — context engineering: frames context as a finite resource and the successor discipline to prompt engineering.
Karpathy — "jagged intelligence": fluent prose is easy; controllable, grounded output is where models are uneven.
PHASE 3Review & Approve — The Gate frontier
Technical depth

Atomic-claim decomposition from fluent text (itself unsolved), evidence binding, NLI entailment, and crucially a calibrated ensemble verifier — not a single LLM judge — with a cost-optimal cascade.

Why it's at the edge — the hottest cluster in AI right now
LLM-as-judge is unreliable: >50% bias-test failure in production; position/verbosity/self-enhancement bias. arXiv 2604.16790 · Adaline. → why one judge isn't enough.
Verifiers get gamed: RLVR reward-hacking (arXiv 2604.15149) and Berkeley RDI benchmark-gaming → verification is adversarial.
"Verification is the bottleneck" — Karpathy, Sequoia Ascent 2026.
Calibration under distribution shift is the named open problem in the routing survey (arXiv 2603.04445) — knowing when to trust the verifier.
Hidden-state probing — predict correctness from internal activations (2605.24919, 2507.16488); a genuinely frontier technique we can layer in.
Cost-optimal routing & cascading (arXiv 2410.10347; RouteLLM) — the "cheap-first, escalate-uncertain" referee design.
PHASE 4Launch & Send applied
Technical depth

Immutable per-claim audit log (claim → source → clearing rule → timestamp) and channel adapters into the existing ESP / sales-engagement stack.

Candor: this phase is systems engineering, not frontier AI — and we present it that way. The only research-adjacent thread is agent observability / the "reproducibility paradox" (record-and-replay of agent runs). Overclaiming a research edge here is exactly what a sharp reviewer would catch; it's load-bearing, not novel.
PHASE 5Optimize — truth-bounded bandit frontier
Technical depth

A contextual bandit per micro-segment over a constrained, verified-only action space; hierarchical sharing for cold-start segments; off-policy evaluation; sparse, delayed, expensive reward; anytime-valid statistics for adaptive tests.

Why it's at the edge
Contextual bandits / RL for LLM decisions — arXiv 2602.03806 (multi-turn code gen); "RL for tool choice" as the missing agent policy.
Off-policy & sparse-reward agentic RL is hard and active — process reward models (arXiv 2504.16828); the reward-scarcity-outside-code gap.
The original contribution: bounding the optimizer's action space to verified outputs is a structural answer to reward-hacking — it directly attacks the RLVR failure mode (2604.15149). "Constrained optimization as anti-Goodhart" is a fresh framing, not just an application — this is the line to lead the technical pitch with.
PHASE 6Monitor & Assure frontier
Technical depth

A claim-dependency graph + targeted re-verification on source change (state/drift), plus an Assurance Lab: synthetic adversarial-trap generation + reliability profiling (catch-rate, false-reject, ECE calibration, severity buckets, paraphrase robustness).

Why it's at the edge
Reliability must be decomposed beyond a single success score — the agent-reliability research line + benchmark-integrity work (Berkeley RDI) argue average success hides consistency / robustness / safety failures.
Synthetic eval generation + verification — the weak-supervision "Mixture of LLMs in the loop" line; synthetic-data verification is what prevents collapse.
Memory / state drift is under-measured — mem0, State of AI Agent Memory 2026.
Honesty note: "ReliabilityBench" / "STATE-Bench" appeared in the cohort research doc, not in my own verification — treat those specific names as directional; the concept (decompose reliability; adversarially test your own verifier) is well-attested.

The line to lead with

"Most AI marketing optimizes for engagement and drifts toward hype. We bound the optimizer to verified claims — a structural fix for the reward-hacking problem the whole field is stuck on."

That sentence is doing the heavy lifting: it names a current, well-cited failure mode (RLVR reward-hacking), and positions the project's most original mechanism (Phase 5's constrained optimization) as the answer — with the verifier (Phase 3) and the assurance lab (Phase 6) as the supporting frontier work.
